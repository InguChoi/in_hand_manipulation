Abstract

Recent advances in reinforcement learning have enabled impressive progress in in-hand manipulation, yet sim-to-real transfer remains a major challenge because of observation noise and complex contact dynamics. In this work, we revisit a fundamental question: does adding more observations improve policy performance?

We conduct a systematic study of observation design for in-hand object reorientation, evaluating multiple policy variants with different combinations of object state, fingertip information, velocity, torque, and contact signals. Surprisingly, richer observations often degrade performance. Velocity and torque signals introduce substantial noise and lead to unstable, oscillatory behavior. Our experiments show that a minimal observation set consisting of object pose and fingertip pose achieves the best performance and stability, highlighting the importance of low-noise, well-structured observations for robust dexterous manipulation.

Method Overview

We study in-hand object reorientation where a robotic hand must rotate a grasped object to match a target orientation. The policy is trained with PPO in Isaac Sim and directly deployed on real hardware through ROS 2, without architectural modification between simulation and deployment.

System

System Overview

Training happens in simulation, and the same frozen policy is copied to the real robotic platform for deployment.

MLP Policy

Policy Representation

An MLP with hidden dimensions [1024, 512, 256, 128] outputs 16 relative joint position targets at every step.

Observations

Observation Ablation

We compare object state, target state, robot state, action history, velocity, torque, and contact variants.

The observation space includes object pose, target orientation and error, joint positions, fingertip pose, and previous action. We then explore how adding or removing velocity, torque, and contact-related signals changes real-world policy behavior.

Analysis and Lessons

Results

We evaluate the learned policy on predefined target orientations using success rate, time-to-success, time-to-failure, and velocity variance. Across these metrics, the vanilla model without velocity achieves the best composite score and the smoothest behavior.

Qualitative Analysis

Highlight stable vanilla rollouts and contrast them with failure cases caused by noisy velocity or torque signals.

Our analysis shows that fingertip pose is critical for performance, while explicit contact signals offer limited benefits. Velocity observations are particularly harmful because numerical differentiation amplifies noise and leads to high-frequency jitter in the control outputs.
